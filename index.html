Key insight
Risk is not randomly distributed — it clusters geographically and diverges structurally, revealing measurable opportunities for targeted managed care intervention. Uniform national strategies fail to capture this heterogeneity.

Analytical findings

Risk concentration

Nearly 1 in 3 counties at high composite risk

29.9% of U.S. counties fall within the high composite risk tier — representing structural exposure rather than isolated outliers. This scale demands a systemic, geographically-informed response rather than case-by-case intervention.

Structural misalignment

28% of counties show severe chronic–social divergence

A 22.6-point mean divergence between chronic burden and SDOH risk scores reveals that clinical and social needs are not co-located. In 28% of counties, this gap exceeds 30 points — indicating populations where social vulnerability significantly outpaces diagnosed clinical burden.

Geographic clustering

High-risk counties cluster into scalable intervention zones

Rather than dispersing randomly, high-risk counties form visible regional clusters — particularly in the Southeast, Appalachia, and parts of the Southwest. This geographic concentration creates natural boundaries for managed care deployment and market-level strategy.

Model validation

Moderate correlation confirms independent domain value

The r≈0.50 correlation between chronic burden and SDOH scores confirms that while the two domains are related, they are not redundant. Each adds independent signal — validating the weighted composite approach over single-domain models.
